Why Your Take-Home Pay Feels Lower Than Your Salary

You just got a job offer for $85,000 a year. That sounds great — until your first paycheck shows up and it's nowhere near $7,083 a month. This calculator tells you what you actually keep after federal income tax and FICA, broken down by week, month, and paycheck period.

Enter your gross salary and filing status, and you'll see your after-tax income across every timeframe. No state taxes, no deductions beyond the standard deduction, no retirement contributions — just the federal number that lets you compare job offers, evaluate a raise, or figure out whether that salary can cover your rent.

Where the Missing Paycheck Money Goes

Gross salary is the number on your offer letter. After-tax salary is the number that hits your bank account. The gap between them is federal income tax plus FICA (Social Security and Medicare).

At $85,000 with single filing status, the standard deduction drops your taxable income to $69,250. Federal income tax on that amount is roughly $10,525. FICA adds another $6,503. Your after-tax salary: $67,972 — about 80% of the gross figure. That 20% gap is the real cost of being an employee.

The gap widens as income rises. At $150,000, you're keeping roughly 75%. At $300,000, it's closer to 71% once the Additional Medicare Tax kicks in above $200,000.

How Federal Income Tax Affects Your Salary

The U.S. uses progressive brackets — not a flat rate. Your first $11,925 of taxable income is taxed at 10%, the next chunk at 12%, then 22%, and so on. This means a raise doesn't push your entire salary into a higher bracket — only the dollars above each threshold get the higher rate.

At $85,000 gross (single, standard deduction), your $69,250 in taxable income crosses three brackets: 10% on the first $11,925, 12% up to $48,475, and 22% on the remainder. The effective rate works out to about 15.2% — far below the 22% marginal rate.

Filing status changes the math significantly. Married filing jointly gets a $31,500 standard deduction and wider bracket thresholds. A married couple earning $85,000 jointly would pay roughly $2,500 less in federal income tax than a single filer at the same salary.

How FICA Changes the Number

FICA is the payroll tax that funds Social Security and Medicare. Unlike income tax, it applies to your gross salary — not taxable income — and there's no standard deduction to reduce it.

  • Social Security (6.2%) applies up to $176,100 in 2025. Earn more than that, and the SS portion stops. At $85,000, you pay $5,270. At $200,000, you still only pay $10,918 on the first $176,100.
  • Medicare (1.45%) has no cap. It applies to every dollar earned.
  • Additional Medicare Tax (0.9%) kicks in above $200,000 for single filers ($250,000 for married filing jointly). This is the surtax that high earners often overlook.

At $85,000, FICA totals $6,503 — about 7.6% of gross salary. At $250,000, FICA rises to roughly $12,845 (5.1% effective), because Social Security has already maxed out but Medicare and the surtax continue climbing.

Your employer pays a matching FICA amount, but that doesn't come out of your paycheck — it's an additional cost to the company on top of your gross salary.

After-Tax Salary vs Net Pay

These terms get used interchangeably, but they're not the same thing.

After-tax salary is gross salary minus federal income tax and FICA. That's what this calculator shows.

Net pay (or take-home pay) is what actually appears in your bank account after everything is subtracted: federal tax, FICA, state income tax, health insurance premiums, retirement contributions (401k, IRA), union dues, garnishments, and any other deductions your employer processes.

This calculator deliberately stops at the federal level. It gives you a clean baseline for comparing salaries across states or job offers without the noise of variable deductions. If you need the full take-home number including state tax and benefits, you'd need a paycheck calculator that models every deduction line.

How to Use These Numbers

Each timeframe answers a different question:

  • Annual — compare total compensation across job offers or years
  • Monthly — match against rent, car payments, and fixed bills
  • Bi-weekly — aligns with most payroll schedules (26 pay periods per year)
  • Weekly — useful for hourly workers or irregular schedules

The bi-weekly figure assumes 26 pay periods. Some years have 27, which can make one paycheck feel like a bonus. If your employer pays semi-monthly (24 periods), divide the annual figure by 24 instead.

These numbers are federal-only. To get actual take-home pay, subtract state income tax, health insurance premiums, and retirement contributions from the after-tax salary shown here.

FAQ

Does this include state tax?

No. This calculator focuses on U.S. federal income tax and optional FICA only. State rates range from 0% (Texas, Florida) to 13.3% (California).

Is this payroll-accurate?

No. It's a planning estimate — it doesn't include payroll deductions, credits, or employer-specific withholding settings.

Can I use this to compare job offers?

Yes. It's built for comparing gross salary to estimated after-tax salary across offers, especially in different states.

Why does my paycheck differ from the estimate?

Actual pay can differ because of state taxes, benefits, retirement contributions, bonuses, pre-tax deductions, and withholding elections.

What's the difference between after-tax salary and net pay?

After-tax salary is gross minus federal tax and FICA. Net pay also subtracts state tax, insurance premiums, retirement contributions, and other deductions. This calculator shows after-tax salary only.

Limitations

Federal tax only. No state or local income tax. State rates range from 0% (Texas, Florida, Washington) to 13.3% (California). This gap can swing take-home pay by thousands.

Standard deduction only. No itemized deductions, no Schedule A. If you have significant mortgage interest, charitable contributions, or state/local tax deductions, your actual federal tax may be lower.

No tax credits. Child Tax Credit, Earned Income Credit, education credits, and other credits that reduce your actual tax bill are not modeled.

No retirement contributions. 401(k), traditional IRA, and HSA contributions reduce taxable income dollar-for-dollar. Not included here.

No pre-tax deductions. Health insurance premiums, flexible spending accounts (FSAs), and commuter benefits all reduce taxable income before federal tax is calculated. Not modeled.

FICA only (employee side). This calculator shows the employee's 6.2% + 1.45%. Self-employed individuals pay both halves (15.3% on 92.35% of net earnings) — use QuarterlyTaxCalc.com for that.

Not a paycheck calculator. Actual take-home pay includes state tax, benefit deductions, and garnishments that this tool doesn't model. Use this for federal planning and salary comparison, not for predicting your bank deposit.
